# GenED-SC: Generative Editing Semantic Communication with Integrated Multi-Modal LLMs 

Shuoyao Wang Senior Member, IEEE , Weisheng Xie, Mingze Gong Student Member, IEEE , Suzhi Bi 

Senior Member, IEEE , and Li Ping Qian Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract — Deep learning-based joint source-channel coding has recently demonstrated strong potential for semantic commu-nication (SemComm). However, most existing approaches focus on optimizing visual-fidelity metrics, which can lead to reduced perceptual quality. Generative model-based SemComm leverages rich prior knowledge from large-scale pre-training to enhance perceptual quality, but often at the cost of increased distortion and unreliability. This paper addresses the above issues by proposing a two-stage semantic image transmission framework, integrating a multimodal large language model (MLLM) for generative editing. In the first stage, a JSCC-based discrimi-native transmission selectively prioritizes semantically important regions, preserving scene layout and object integrity under lim-ited bandwidth. In the second phase, MLLM-driven generative editing refines missing details based on the textual descriptions, enhancing semantic fidelity and perceptual quality. Extensive experiments show that the proposed framework achieves state-of-the-art performance in semantic preservation, perceptual quality, and visual fidelity across a wide range of channel conditions, especially in low-SNR regimes. 

Index Terms —Semantic communication, image transmission, multimodal large language model. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The sixth generation (6G) is envisioned as an intelligent information system, both powered by and driving advances in deep learning (DL) technology. Leveraging DL breakthroughs, semantic communication (SemComm) systems typically em-ploy deep neural networks (DNNs) for joint source-channel coding (JSCC), enabling efficient extraction, encoding, and transmission of semantic information across diverse data types, including images and text. In particular, the representative DeepJSCC [1] jointly trains neural encoders and decoders to learn robust feature mappings integrating source and channel coding, thereby achieving graceful performance degradation with SNR and outperforming conventional schemes. Despite the superior performance of current DL-based JSCC systems in semantic image transmission, they are typically optimized for pixel-level fidelity between the source and reconstructed images, often measured by metrics such as peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR). However, it is increasingly acknowledged that high pixel-level similarity does not nec-essarily translate into high perceptual quality, which better captures the realism and visual appeal as perceived by human observers [2]. To enhance the perceptual quality of transmitted images, generative AI (GenAI) models have recently achieved re-markable success in enabling communication at the semantic level. Rather than transmitting dense pixel-level features, these approaches exploited generative models (e.g., Stable Diffu-sion [3]) to encode and send compact, high-level cues, such as image captions [4], object layouts [5], or semantic segmen-tation maps [6], that emphasize semantically critical content while discarding redundant visual details. At the receiver, a generative model synthesizes the final image conditioned on these cues, i.e. conditional generation, enabling more efficient bandwidth utilization and greater flexibility in adapting to task-specific requirements. Leveraging rich prior knowledge from large-scale pre-training, generative SemComm often produces results that are perceptually rich. Despite these advances, generative SemComm still faces two main challenges: i) The output quality is highly sensitive to the precision of the transmitted conditions. Using fine-grained cues, such as segmentation maps [7] and latent em-beddings [8], substantially increases communication overhead. Conversely, relying on coarse-grained semantic conditions (e.g., class labels or captions) fails to capture sufficient visual details, leading to noticeable mismatches in spatial layout, color fidelity, and textural consistency between the recon-structed and original images [4]. ii) Due to the cross-modal predictive nature, generative rendering suffers from inherent randomness in rendering outcomes [9]. Overall, achieving high-performance generative SemComm requires addressing two fundamental open questions:  

> •

What semantic conditions should be transmitted to bal-ance communication efficiency and generation quality?  

> •

How can condition transmission be optimized over wire-less channels, given that large-scale generative models are typically used as frozen functions, where finetuning over wireless channels in an end-to-end manner is impractical due to the huge amount of tunable parameters? For Q1, recent advances in multimodal large language mod-els (MLLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in both cross-modal generation and fine-grained image editing 1.Inspired by this, we propose Generative Editing Semantic Communication (GenED-SC), a novel paradigm that enables efficient semantic image transmission and controllable content refinement. Unlike prior Generative SemComm approaches that mainly focus on generative rendering (i.e., cross-modal generation), we are among the first to explore a generative editing framework that integrates discriminative and gener-ative processes. This editing-based approach offers distinct advantages by preserving the structural fidelity of the original

For Q2, we argue that achieving higher pixel fidelity via conventional JSCC does not necessarily lead to better per-ceptual quality, as it often wastes bandwidth on semantically redundant regions (e.g., background). To this end, we develop a two-stage semantic density-aware framework. Here, “seman-tic density” refers to the spatial concentration of semantically important information within an image, quantified through feature-level importance scores derived from multimodal se-mantic alignment. In Stage I, a discriminative JSCC encoder performs semantically-aware compression. It prioritizes the transmission of crucial foreground details while aggressively compressing less important background regions, significantly reducing data transmission under limited bandwidth without sacrificing structural integrity. In Stage II, a pre-trained mul-timodal LLM performs generative refinement. It hallucinates plausible details for the compressed background based on the accurately transmitted layout and semantic context, thereby enhancing the overall perceptual quality while maintaining consistency. Overall, this hybrid discriminative-generative paradigm achieves a superior trade-off among semantic fidelity, percep-tual quality, and visual fidelity compared to conventional ap-proaches. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:  

> •

A hybrid generative editing semantic communica-tion framework (GenED-SC): We propose a hy-brid discriminative-generative semantic communication framework that integrates a semantic-aware JSCC trans-mission front-end with a frozen MLLM-based generative refinement back-end. By adopting a generative editing paradigm rather than direct cross-modal rendering, the proposed framework preserves source structure while en-abling controllable semantic refinement, thereby improv-ing the trade-off among transmission efficiency, semantic fidelity, and perceptual quality.  

> •

To address the unequal importance of different image regions in downstream generative editing, we propose a foreground/background dual-branch transmission archi-tecture that allocates communication resources differently to semantically important and redundant regions. To sup-port this decomposition, we further introduce a semantic importance prediction module for explicit semantic-pixel alignment, which guides the separation of transmitted representations into foreground-detail and background-context branches.  

> •

To address the infeasibility of end-to-end training, we introduce a semantic-importance-aware JSCC objective that optimizes the transmission front-end for downstream generative editing rather than conventional pixel-level reconstruction alone. The proposed loss function jointly considers pixel-semantic matching, foreground detail re-construction, and background semantic preservation, en-abling a superior trade-off among semantic fidelity, per-ceptual quality, and visual realism.  

> •

Extensive experiments demonstrate that GenED-SC con-sistently outperforms state-of-the-art discriminative JSCC and generative semantic communication baselines under matched settings. The results verify superior semantic fidelity, perceptual quality, and bandwidth efficiency, in-cluding up to 42.5% improvement in perceptual quality metrics. In this section, we first review the evolution of DeepJSCC-based SemComm approaches. We then focus on generative SemComm. Finally, we discuss LLM-aided approaches, in-cluding both discriminative JSCC and generative SemComm. 

A. Discriminative SemComm 

As an emerging communication paradigm, SemComm aims to enhance communication efficiency through artificial intelli-gence technologies DeepJSCC [1] pioneered this paradigm by jointly learning source and channel coding with DNN-based autoencoders. Inspired by the great success of diffusion models, recent research has further incorporated diffusion-based enhance-ment modules into the Deep learning-based JSCC approaches. A straightforward approach is to preserve the DNN-based encoder-decoder structure while introducing diffusion-based denoising in the latent space, thereby leveraging the prior knowledge of generative models to improve robustness and noise resilience [10]–[12]. However, discriminative decoders that focus primarily on visual fidelity still suffer from degraded perceptual quality, even when the latent features are refined. 

B. Generative SemComm 

Generative SemComm primarily includes methods based on generative adversarial networks (GANs) and diffusion models. In these approaches, the reconstruction process at the receiver side employs generative models, which leverage rich prior knowledge from large-scale pre-training to enhance perceptual quality. In GAN-based SemComm, Han et al. [13] utilized GANs to achieve semantic image transmission with high compression ratios while considering privacy protection, although this approach required a complex training process. However, similar to GAN-based methods in image processing (e.g., super-resolution, deblurring), the training of GAN-based SemComm models [13], [14] is unstable and prone to mode collapse, resulting in repetitive or highly similar generated samples that compromise image quality and diversity. In contrast, diffusion models provide significant advantages in terms of generation quality and training stability. For example, Park et al. [4] proposed transmitting only textual descriptions of the source image, which are then decoded into images via pretrained text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models. While [4] significantly reduces communication overhead, it often suffers from high stochasticity and poor structural con-sistency due to the inherent ambiguity of language prompts. Accordingly, subsequent works incorporate auxiliary visual priors as conditions for generation: i) Spatial layout cues such as coarse masks [5] or edge maps [15] to localize main objects and maintain the overall structure; ii) Semantic segmentation maps [7] to preserve object boundaries and scene composition; iii) Symbolic or relational priors, e.g. scene graphs [16] or agent-local maps [17], to recover high-level structured remote scenes. These limitations reveal a critical trade-off: while richer condition signals, such as segmentation or depth maps, can boost reconstruction, they introduce substantial communica-tion overhead, undermining the bandwidth efficiency that SemComm aims to achieve. Conversely, lightweight condi-tions reduce transmission cost but fail to convey sufficient structural guidance, leading to semantic ambiguity and visual artifacts. Thus, achieving high-fidelity, controllable image re-construction under strict bandwidth constraints remains a key challenge. A related approach addressing a similar bandwidth-fidelity trade-off is the concurrent Mixture of Semantics (MoS) scheme [18], which seeks a balance by decomposing the image into regions of interest and non-interest for prioritized and lightweight transmission, respectively. 

C. LLM-based SemComm 

In addition to adopting generative paradigms for image reconstruction, another trend in SemComm is the use of LLMs to enhance semantic fidelity, robustness, and adaptability. Ex-isting LLM-based SemComm studies can be broadly grouped into two categories. 

1) Semantic Importance Indicator: These works integrate LLM reasoning into the source-channel coding process at the transmitter side, to improve compression efficiency and resilience against channel impairments. For instance, [19] uses a pre-trained LLM to quantifie frame-level semantic importance for power allocation, while [20] integrates LLM tokenizers with unsupervised pre-training to construct a se-mantic knowledge base for improved decoding. [21] treats LLM as a semantic codec, combining subword tokenization, adaptive rate control, and private-knowledge fine-tuning for robust transmission. LaMoSC [22] further positions LLM as a “semantic hub” that fuses visual–text cues for joint source– channel design, yielding better image quality at low SNR. [23] develops a LLM foundation-model-driven framework with perception-error-aware semantic power allocation, adapting transmission energy to cue importance for generative recon-struction. 

2) Text Correction: While the above methods improve efficiency and robustness, they still suffer from the semantic inconsistency and reconstruction degradation at the receiver side caused by the noisy wireless channel. To address this, a growing body of work employs LLMs as semantic mediators ,not merely as compressors or classifiers, but as intelligent agents that construct, refine, or repair textual prompts. For instance, [24] leverages a LLM to refine textual descriptions via irrelevant-token filtering and contextual similarity, and [25] enables privacy-preserving multimodal interpretation without exposing raw data. Ref. [26] combines BLIP-based text extrac-tion with Stable Diffusion, enhanced by BART text correction and continual learning for improved stability. Prompt-based designs, such as [27] and [28], jointly optimize semantic en-coding and context-aware prompt refinement, transmitting only filtered semantic cues for high-quality, low-bandwidth AIGC reconstruction. The progressive scheme in [29] transmits text incrementally, allowing LLM-based editing and completion when packets are lost before final image synthesis. 

3) This work: Unlike prior works that primarily leverage LLMs for image/text understanding or textual refinement, we propose utilizing MLLMs for image generative editing. In our framework, the MLLM acts as an intelligent image editor, performing guided inpainting based on a semantically weighted and spatially faithful prior, thereby preserving fine structural details while enriching local textures and global con-text through LLM-derived knowledge. This work introduces a novel paradigm for enhancing the efficiency of semantic communication by harnessing the powerful generative and reasoning capabilities of MLLMs. III. S YSTEM MODEL

A. CLIP-Based Cross-Modal Context Matching Module 

As shown in Fig. 3, given an input image Io and a target class text T (e.g., “Dog”), we utilize a pre-trained CLIP model [34] to extract semantic-aligned visual and textual features. Specifically, the CLIP image encoder outputs the global feature g ∈ RC and the patch-level visual embedding 

ev ∈ RC×H×W . The CLIP text encoder generates the class-level text embedding et ∈ RC .To bridge the gap between generic text descriptions and pixel-level image content, we refine et into a context-aware representation ˜et by fusing it with the aggregated visual context [g; ev] via a cross-modal transformer decoder. This process yields a refined semantic vector that is specifically attuned to the input image, effectively pinpointing the latent visual attributes that correspond to the text prompt T .The similarity between ˜et and each spatial location in 

ev is then calculated to generate the semantic importance score map S ∈ RH×W . This yields a spatially varying prior that highlights semantically salient regions corresponding to 

T , providing precise, pixel-level guidance for the subsequent routing module. Complete computational details are delineated in Appendix A.

Note: The saliency maps Msal are used only during training to provide weak supervision for the score map. During infer-ence, our framework operates in a fully self-guided manner without requiring any external annotations or saliency inputs. 

D. MLLM-based Semantic Editing for Image Reconstruction 

To further MLLMs for semantic enhancement and back-ground synthesis, we adopt a generative editing pipeline that operates on the JSCC-reconstructed image Ir . Given the imperfect but semantically aligned reconstruction from the GenED-SC encoder, our goal is to preserve the target foreground content while inpainting or replacing background regions based on a text prompt. In particular, we first develop the following prompt to achieve I2T in the transmitter: 

Prompt: 

Describe the image in precise detail to enable accurate text-to-image generation. First, summarize the overall scene and tone. Next, identify all main objects, including their quantities, colors, and appearances. Then, specify the absolute position (e.g., top, bottom, left, right) of each object and the relative spatial relationships between them (e.g., above, below, in front of, behind). Finally, describe how these objects are positioned within the background scene. Provide a comprehensive, de-tailed, and accurate description to ensure the generated image faithfully reconstructs the original. 

Moreover, we design a task-specific prompt to guide the MLLM in editing the image in the receiver: 

Prompt: 

This is a compressed image. Please enhance the resolution and sharpness of this image while preserving its original colors, details, composition, and layout. The restored image should look clear and high-definition. Please control the brightness and average brightness of each part of the generated image to be consistent with the reference image I gave. Use the following text to reconstruct: {Caption from the image }.

This prompt explicitly instructs the model to maintain the identity and structure of the foreground while regenerating the background based on a textual description (e.g., scene, context, or style). This design aligns with our overarching goal: to minimize information redundancy in transmission by selectively encoding foreground content via JSCC, while offloading the reconstruction of background details to a text-guided generative model. By combining the strengths of semantic-aware compression and text-conditioned generation, our framework achieves faith-ful preservation of critical image regions and flexible back-ground synthesis, enabling a new paradigm for bandwidth-efficient semantic image communication. A. Experiment Setups 1) Datasets: We use the DUTS salient object detection dataset and the Pascal VOC dataset as the experimental data for training and testing. The DUTS dataset includes DUTS-TR with 10,553 images for training and DUTS-TE with 5,019 images for testing. The Pascal VOC dataset consists of 10,000 images, divided into training and testing sets with a 9:1 ratio. All images are resized to 256 × 256 pixels during pre-processing. The ground-truth saliency masks are manually annotated to highlight the most visually prominent objects in each scene, guided by human visual attention. Notably, DUTS contains diverse scenes, including cases with multiple prominent objects, rather than only single-object images. Therefore, the learned semantic importance predictor is trained with saliency patterns beyond simple single-object settings. The DUTS-TR annotations serve solely as weak se-mantic priors for training our semantic importance prediction module. During inference, the semantic importance predictor directly estimates the foreground/background mask from the input features, without requiring saliency annotations or the class-level text label T .

2) Training Settings: Without loss of generality, we con-sider AWGN channel as the wireless channel in the following training and evaluation. During the training stage, the channel SNR for each iteration is randomly selected from {1, 4, 7, 10 } dB, and the compression ratio for each image is 1/24 

to align with SGDJSCC [11]. Note that, the entire discrim-inative transmission branch—comprising the visual feature encoder, semantic routing module, dual-branch encoder, and corresponding decoders—is jointly optimized in an end-to-end manner without separate or staged training for the foreground and background branches. The model is trained for 2000 epochs using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 

10 −4. The model is trained on an NVIDIA RTX4090 in Ubuntu 20.04 LTS with Intel® Xeon® Platinum 8336C CPU @2.30GHz, and evaluate the performance of the converged model results under the same and sufficient training epochs. The hyperparameters for the composite loss function are empirically set to λ1 = 1 .0, λ2 = 0 .0001 , and λ3 = 0 .01 

to balance semantic alignment, foreground fidelity, and back-ground consistency. In addition, the Gumbel-Softmax temper-ature is set to 1.0, and the contextual-loss temperature is set to τ = 0 .5. Unless otherwise specified, the above setting is used as the default training configuration. For experiments in-volving different operating points (e.g., different compression ratios/bandwidth settings), retraining is performed separately for each operating point. In order to restore the background texture and other infor-mation as much as possible, we used GPT4 (OpenAI, model: gpt-4o-2024-05-13) to generate image captions so that suffi-cient prompt information is available for editing and restora-tion under the highly compressed background. To ensure fair comparison across all baselines involving textual inputs, we adopt a standardized and lossless text transmission scheme. Specifically, all text prompts are encoded using UTF-8 and quantized into binary bitstreams. The resulting sequences are protected using a systematic LDPC encoder with a fixed code rate of 0.5, ensuring reliable transmission over noisy channels without loss of semantic information.

well aligned with the objective of generative semantic com-munication , where the receiver aims to reconstruct images that preserve the transmitted semantics while maintaining high perceptual quality, rather than reproducing the source image in a strictly pixel-wise manner. This choice is also consistent with recent literature. For example, [18] employ a CLIP-based semantic metric, and [15] also adopt CLIP-based evaluation for semantic reconstruction quality. Perceptual metrics are likewise widely used in related works: [4] report LPIPS, while [37] evaluate reconstruction quality using LPIPS together with other perceptual and structural measures. Therefore, the combination of CLIP-Score, BRISQUE, and LPIPS provides a more com-prehensive and task-relevant evaluation of both the proposed method and the benchmark schemes.

F. Failure Analysis and Limitations 

Although GenED-SC consistently outperforms both dis-criminative and generative baselines, limitations still arise under extremely challenging communication conditions. Fig-ure 12 presents representative cases in the ultra-low-bandwidth and low-SNR regime ( CR = 1 /48 , SNR = 1 dB), which are intentionally chosen to stress-test the robustness limit of the proposed framework. These examples should therefore be interpreted as boundary-case behaviors of generative semantic communication under severe transmission degradation, rather than typical operating results. As highlighted by the red boxes in Fig. 12, the domi-nant limitation is localized semantic inconsistency rather than global structural collapse. When the transmitted sketch prior is heavily corrupted by aggressive compression and channel noise, the downstream MLLM may receive insufficient local structural evidence for fine-grained object understanding, lead-ing to ambiguity-driven distortions in semantically uncertain regions. A further source of error is text-image mismatch, where degraded textual guidance or incomplete alignment between text and visual structure causes the edited result to deviate from the intended semantics. Importantly, these cases also reveal the key advantage of the proposed hybrid design. Even under such extreme conditions, GenED-SC still tends to preserve the overall scene layout, coarse object structure, and spatial composition better than the baseline generative methods. VI. C ONCLUSION 

In this paper, we proposed a semantic density-aware multi-modal large language model-edited image transmission frame-work, named GenED-SC. In particular, the proposed genera-tive editing semantic communication consists of two stages. In the first phase, a JSCC-based discriminative transmission selectively prioritizes semantically critical regions, preserving global scene structure and object integrity under bandwidth constraints. In the second phase, MLLM-driven generative editing refines missing or degraded details by leveraging textual semantic priors, thereby enhancing both semantic con-sistency and perceptual quality. Extensive simulations on the DUTS dataset have validated the effectiveness of our method in achieving high semantic fidelity, perceptual quality, and robustness across a wide range of SNRs. The outstanding per-formance achived by GenED-SC demonstrates that bridging the semantic and pixel domains enables both interpretability and efficiency in image communication. Future work will extend this framework to video transmission and explore user-controllable editing for downstream applications. APPENDIX
