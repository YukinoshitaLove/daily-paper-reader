## Abstract 

Score-based diffusion models in infinite-dimensional function spaces provide a mathe-matically principled framework for modelling function-valued data, offering key advantages such as resolution invariance and the ability to handle irregular discretisations. However, practical implementations have struggled to fully realise these benefits. Existing backbones like Fourier neural operators are often biased towards regular grids and fail to generalise to complex domain topologies. We propose a novel architecture for function-space diffusion models that represents generalised graph convolutional kernels as finite element functions, enabling the model to naturally handle unstructured meshes and complex geometries. We demonstrate the efficacy of our network architecture through a series of unconditional and conditional sampling experiments across diverse geometries, including non-convex and multiply-connected domains. Our results show that the proposed method maintains resolution invariance and achieves high fidelity in capturing functional distributions on non-trivial geometries.

Instead of modelling finite-dimensional vectors, infinite-dimensional diffusion models learn distributions directly over functions. There are two main approaches for applying diffusion mod-els to function-valued data. The first is the discretise-first 

approach, where functions are discretised onto a fixed grid and standard finite-dimensional diffusion models are ap-plied. The second is the discretise-last approach, where diffusion models are formulated directly in function space and discretisation is introduced only at training or sampling time. This provides several desirable properties: 1. Resolution invariance : Models trained at one resolu-tion can generalise to different resolutions. 2. Handling irregular data : Models naturally support unstructured meshes and complex geometries. 3. Theoretical guarantees: Convergence results hold independently of the discretisation resolution. Furthermore, modelling directly in function spaces allows incorporating prior knowledge about the structure of the data, such as smoothness or Hilbert space constraints, which can improve both modelling flexibility and theoretical con-vergence guarantees (Pidstrigach et al., 2024). Despite these advantages, existing numerical implementa-tions do not fully realise these theoretical benefits. Convo-lutional U-Net architectures perform well on fixed, regular grids but lack resolution invariance and struggle with irregu-lar geometries (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Hagemann et al., 2025). Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs) (Li et al., 2021), on the other hand, provide resolution invariance but do not naturally handle irregular geometries or even non-regular grids. There is still a gap between the theoretical promises of infinite-dimensional diffusion models and practical archi-tectures that fully exploit these promises.

## 3. Architecture for Infinite-Dimensional Diffusion Models 

3.1. Desirable Properties for the Diffusion Network 

A central challenge in the infinite-dimensional framework is to parametrise the score function as a neural operator 

sθ : [0 , T ] × H → H that maps between function spaces (Kovachki et al., 2023). While neural operators are most prominently used to learn solution operators of PDEs (Aziz-zadenesheli et al., 2024), they provide a natural backbone for parametrising the infinite-dimensional score. To fully leverage the infinite-dimensional framework on irregular data, the score network should exhibit four key properties: 1. Multiscale processing: Local fine-scale features and global structures must be captured, analogous to U-Nets. 2. Resolution and discretisation invariance: The oper-ator must define a mapping between function spaces. A model trained at resolution N must generalise to 

N ′̸ = N without retraining. 3. Domain flexibility: The architecture must be able to encode and decode functions on arbitrary and complex domain geometries without structural modification. 4. Global structure: The model should capture and main-tain global information about the target distribution when evaluating the score. The last point is essential, since the score function depends on the full input configuration, not only on local features. Architectures based solely on message passing propagate information through local interactions and therefore, lack an explicit global state (unless augmented with pooling or hierarchical mechanisms). As a result, they may struggle to capture long-range dependencies and global structure. 

3.2. Existing Neural Operator Architectures Grid-based neural operators Standard U-Nets satisfy Property 1 but fail Property 2 and Property 3, as convolu-tions are restricted to fixed regular grids. A widespread class of neural operators similarly relies on regular grids, includ-ing CNO (Raonic et al., 2023) and FNO (Li et al., 2021), which satisfy Property 1 and Property 2 up to spectral trun-cation but fail Property 3 on irregular geometries (Bartolucci et al., 2023). While adaptations exist, e.g., domain masking, non-uniform Fourier transforms (Lingsch et al., 2023), or spatial deformation maps (Li et al., 2023), they introduce computational overhead and additional training complexity.

Remark 3.1 . The construction extends naturally to d-dimensional domains by replacing the Q1 basis with a d-fold multilinear basis on a P ×d reference grid, giving P d filter weights and at most 2d nonzero basis overlaps per projected neighbour. While this is tractable for the 2 or 3-dimensional domains, which are considered in recent applications (Yao et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2026), for d ≫ 3

alternative construction have to be considered, e.g., based on sparse-grids (Bungartz & Griebel, 2004). This parametrisation offers three advantages over MLP or spline kernels. First, it is a strict generalisation of the stan-dard discrete CNN. On a uniform Cartesian mesh with r

## 5. Gaussian Blobs 

We first consider the Gaussian Blobs dataset to study the resolution invariance of our model as well as its generalisa-tion to different geometries. The Gaussian blob dataset is created using 1 ≤ N ≤ 3 elliptical Gaussian distributions (see Section D.3). In our implementation, we discretise the continuous field as a piecewise constant function defined over the centroid dual graph of a regular triangulation of the domain (i.e., P0 elements). 

5.1. Results Resolution Invariance We evaluate resolution invariance by training all baseline models on a coarse discretisation (2048 elements) and sampling on a finer one ( 8192 ele-ments) with roughly the same number of parameters, see Table 3. In particular, we consider a square domain to enable direct comparisons with CNN- and FNO-based baselines. Figure 3 summarises the results. All methods generate plau-sible samples at the resolution observed during training. Clear differences emerge when sampling at a higher resolu-tion. Similarly, GAOT struggles to generalise to the finer mesh and produces visible artifacts. In contrast, both the FNO and our GRIFDIR produce re-alistic samples at both the training and target resolutions, demonstrating strong resolution invariance. ## 6. Pinball dataset 

The pinball dataset (Tomasetto et al., 2025) consists of two-dimensional fluid flow simulations around a collection of three (rotating) circular obstacles (“pinballs”). Each simu-lation is parametrised by the velocity vector μ ∈ R3 and evolves over a physical time horizon τ ∈ [0 , 3] , see Ap-pendix D.4 for a detailed description. The dataset exhibits a rich structure, as varying the velocity μ of the pinballs perturbs the solution in qualitatively different ways. In our implementation, we discretise the data as a piecewise linear function defined on mesh vertices. We train two variations of our diffusion model on this dataset: 1. Unconditional model: We collect rollouts across dif-ferent values of μ into a single large dataset and train a diffusion model without any conditional information. The node features consists of the noisy signal at and the diffusion time t.2. Conditional model: We train a diffusion model con-ditioned both on the physical simulation time τ and the velocity μ. The conditional information is incorpo-rated by appending [τ, μ] to the initial node features.

## 7. Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced GRIFDIR, a multi-scale graph neural operator making use of FEM convolution, designed for generative modelling over irregular geometries. By construction, GRIFDIR is discretisation invariant and sup-ports sampling at arbitrary resolution, making it well-suited as a backbone for score-based diffusion models defined in infinite-dimensional function spaces. We demonstrated the effectiveness of our approach across two datasets span-ning a variety of domains, e.g., including squared, cross-shaped, and L-shaped geometries, consistently producing high-quality samples without retraining across resolutions. Several directions remain for future work. On the condi-tional generation side, we plan to extend our framework to non-linear forward operators and to benchmark against learned guidance approaches such as conditional training (Baldassari et al., 2023) and supervised guidance methods like SGT (Baker et al., 2026). Further, ablation regard-ing the model architecture and the underlying the infinite-dimensional framework, such as the choice of covariance 

C or SDE, would be valuable. We also intend to broaden 8GRIFDIR: Graph Resolution-Invariant FEM Diffusion Models in Function Spaces over Irregular Domains 

our comparisons to include other graph neural operator ar-chitectures that can serve as backbones within diffusion frameworks. Finally, we note that comparisons to recent work in functional flow matching, for example MINO (Shi et al., 2025), which is in principle adaptable to diffusion-based modelling, are of particular interest.

## A. Related Work 

Graph Diffusion It is important to distinguish the infinite-dimensional framework from recent works that apply GNNs within standard finite-dimensional diffusion models. For instance, Valencia et al. (2025) propose Graph Diffusion Models to learn the conditional distribution of a fluid’s equilibrium state given a short simulation rollout. While this approach uses GNNs for diffusion, it operates within the finite-dimensional framework on discrete states corrupted by i.i.d. Gaussian noise. By contrast, our infinite-dimensional formulation ensures the diffusion model operates in a resolution-invariant manner by design. 

Functional Flow Matching Functional Flow Matching (FFM) (Kerrigan et al., 2024) is a related framework to infinite-dimensional diffusion. FFM generalises flow matching to operate in infinite-dimensional function spaces. Rather than learning a vector field on a finite-dimensional latent, FFM defines a path of probability measures interpolating between a Gaussian measure (often N (0 , C )) and the data distribution, and learns a function-space vector field that generates this path. As in our setting, extending such generative models to function spaces requires discretisation-invariant network architectures capable of operating on irregular domains. This need is addressed by MINO (Shi et al., 2025), which makes use of graph neural operators and cross-attention mechanisms. Our work shares this motivation: the infinite-dimensional diffusion model we develop similarly requires mesh-aware architectures to handle unstructured spatial domains, and the resulting model is likewise discretisation-invariant by design.

## C. GRIFDIR Architecture Details 

We parametrise the score network sθ (t, a ) as a multi-resolution graph U-Net whose intra-level operator is the FEM-basis convolution of Equation (14) . We describe the forward pass; encoder, V-cycle across L mesh resolutions, global latent transformer at the coarsest level, and decoder and embedded supporting components (FiLM timestep conditioning, learned restriction/prolongation, position re-injection, multi-domain heads). 

Mesh hierarchy. Meshes are built once per domain and cached. For square conductivity ( 32 × 32 DG-0 cells) we use a uniform refinement chain 32 → 16 → 8 → 4 and store sparse pool/unpool matrices per level. D.1. Baseline Approaches MP-GNN GNN based on the message passing framework, modeled after the MP-PDE Solver (Brandstetter et al., 2022). Each node corresponds to a spatial point on the mesh, and edges encode local spatial connectivity. For each edge (i, j ), the message is computed from the hidden states hi, h j , the difference in input values ai − aj , and the relative position xi − xj :

 [hi, h j , a i − aj , x i − xj ]

where ϕm is a two-layer MLP with Swish activations. Using differences rather than raw values makes the messages translation-equivariant in both signal and position space. Aggregated messages (mean pooling) are combined with the current hidden state via a residual connection:

followed by instance normalization. The time step t is embedded via a Fourier feature embedding and concatenated with the node features in the encoder, allowing the network to function as a time-conditioned score network sθ (a, t, x ) for diffusion-based generative modelling over function spaces. The configuration used hidden dimension 128 , 4 message-passing layers, mean aggregation, time embedding dimension equal to the hidden dimension. 

FNO Fourier Neural Operator (Li et al., 2021) parametrises the score in the spectral domain. We consider the fluid pinball setup from Tomasetto et al. (2025) (Section D.). The pinball example involves a 2D advection-diffusion problem with an implicit parametric dependence. In particular, they consider a (incompressible) fluid in a square domain [−1, 1] 2 with three rotating cylinders centered at (−0.5, −0.5) , (0 .5, −0.5) and (0 .0, 0.5) with radius 0.15 . The constant rotation is given by velocities μ = [ v1, v 2, v 3]. The rotation leads to a fluid motion inside the square domain, where the velocity v : [ −1, 1] 2 → R2 and pressure p : [ −1, 1] 2 → R are determinates by the (steady-state) Navier-Stokes equation. Tomasetto et al. (2025) use viscosity ν = 1 .0 and no-slip boundary conditions on the external walls and Dirichlet boundary conditions on the cylinders. They then consider a quantity (e.g., mass or particle density) ρ : [ −1, 1] 2 × [0 , T ] → R that spreads inside the domain according to 

ρt + ∇ · (−η∇ρ + v(μ)y) = 0 , (35) with homogeneous Neumann boundary conditions, η = 0 .001 , T = 3 and v(μ) as the solution of the Navier-Stokes equation given the velocity of the cylinders μ. For a fixed initial condition

We collect roll-outs of ρt for 400/50/50 varying μ as our training/validation/test sets and aim to learn this distribution (independent of t).
