Abstract. Remote Sensing (RS) single-image super-resolution aims to reconstruct high-resolution imagery from low-resolution observations while preserving fine spatial structures. Recent Swin Transformer-based mod-els, including Swin2SR, provide strong spatial context modeling through shifted-window self-attention, but their feed-forward networks remain generic channel-mixing modules and do not separate low-frequency struc-tural content from high-frequency residual detail. To address this limita-tion, we propose SFG-SwinSR, a Spatial-Frequency Gated Swin Trans-former for single-image super-resolution in remote sensing. SFG-SwinSR modifies the original Swin2SR attention block by replacing each trans-former block’s standard feed-forward network with a lightweight Spatial-Frequency Gated Feed-Forward Network (SFG-FFN). The module esti-mates low-frequency content via a depthwise-blur branch, extracts high-frequency residuals by subtraction, refines them with a lightweight spa-tial branch, and adaptively injects detail through a bottleneck gate. Ex-periments on SpaceNet and SEN2VEN μS show that SFG-SwinSR im-proves reconstruction quality under the evaluated settings. On SpaceNet, it achieves 45.19 dB PSNR and 0.9852 SSIM, indicating effective en-hancement of high-frequency details. This demonstrates that spatial-frequency transformation within the transformer feed-forward network improves detail reconstruction in RS super-resolution. 

Keywords: Remote sensing super-resolution · Swin Transformer · spatial-frequency gating · feed-forward network · high-frequency detail recovery 

# 1 Introduction 

High-resolution remote sensing imagery is essential for accurate Earth obser-vation, supporting applications such as urban mapping, precision agriculture, environmental monitoring, infrastructure analysis, and disaster management. Nevertheless, the spatial resolution of satellite images is limited by sensor char-acteristics, imaging optics, orbital geometry, atmospheric conditions, and sensor-induced artifacts such as stripe noise [8]. These limitations, along with blur and

downsampling, suppress high-frequency spatial details and degrade fine struc-tures such as narrow roads, building outlines, field boundaries, and land-cover transitions. Therefore, recovering such fine spatial details through remote sens-ing single-image super-resolution (RS-SISR) remains an important and active research problem. Early deep learning approaches for RS-SISR predominantly relied on CNNs (Convolutional Neural Networks) [14, 20], but there is a cur-rent trend toward the use of transformer based architectures like Swin Trans-former [11, 21, 23] because of their ability to capture long range dependencies efficiently. In addition, recent studies on real world RS-SISR emphasize the need for degradation aware training and robust benchmark design to allow the effec-tive reconstruction of fine detail [25]. In remote sensing imagery, high-frequency information is highly discrimi-native for object delineation and the preservation of land-cover boundaries. Accurate reconstruction of edges, textures, roads, rooftops, and field bound-aries is therefore important for both visual quality and downstream analy-sis [4, 19]. However, although Transformer-based models improve contextual modeling, their feature transformation stages are typically implemented using generic feed-forward networks (FFNs). These FFNs do not explicitly distinguish between low-frequency structural content and high-frequency residual detail, which can limit fine-detail recovery in remote sensing super-resolution. To address this limitation, we propose a Spatial-Frequency Gated Swin Trans-former (SFG-SwinSR). We have modified the Swin2SR [2] self-attention block by replacing the standard FFN in each transformer block with a lightweight Spatial-Frequency Gated Feed-Forward Network (SFG-FFN) 6. The proposed module estimates low-frequency content using a depthwise blur operator, de-rives a high-frequency residual by subtraction, refines the residual through a lightweight spatial branch, and adaptively injects useful detail through a bot-tleneck gating mechanism. This localized architectural modification preserves the long-range dependency modeling capability of Swin2SR while introducing explicit spatial-frequency feature transformation inside the FFN. Experiments on SpaceNet and SEN2VEN μS test pairs show that SFG-SwinSR consistently outperforms the Swin2SR baseline and provides a modest but consistent gain over the strongest Swin2SR configuration under structure-aware supervision. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 1. We propose a Spatial-Frequency Gated Feed-Forward Network (SFG-FFN) that separates low-frequency structural content and high-frequency residual detail inside Swin Transformer blocks for remote sensing super-resolution. 2. We introduce a lightweight residual-detail refinement and adaptive bottle-neck gating mechanism that improves fine-detail reconstruction by modifying the original Swin2SR attention block. 3. We evaluate SFG-SwinSR against Swin2SR under multiple supervision set-tings and show consistent improvement over the L1-trained baseline and a modest gain over the strongest Swin2SR configuration.

# 2 Related Work 

CNN-based Image Super-Resolution: Single-image super-resolution (SR) has been widely explored using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The sem-inal study of SRCNN [3] showed that end-to-end-trained mappings significantly surpass traditional interpolation methods. Later, models such as VDSR [12] and EDSR [16] leveraged deep network architectures and residual learning paradigms to enhance reconstruction performance. Further progress was made using RDN [27] and RCAN [26], which harnessed dense hierarchical reuse of features and chan-nel attention, respectively. In particular, RCAN illustrated that the skip connec-tion of rich low-frequency details via residual connections enables the network to dedicate more resources for modeling informative high-frequency details. This observation is closely related to the motivation of our proposed spatial-frequency feature transformation. As CNN models become highly successful, they inher-ently have limitations associated with their smaller receptive field sizes, limiting their effectiveness in detecting spatial dependencies at larger distances. In the case of satellite imagery, this is a critical issue to consider. 

Remote-Sensing Image Super-Resolution: The field of remote sensing SR has seen advancements through various deep learning models, from task-oriented pipelines to more advanced architectures. Various surveys have been carried out in this respect, which show that several challenges remain, namely issues associated with preserving textures, consistency of geometric features, and domain discrepancy between the synthetic degradation models used and the ac-tual sensing process [5, 19]. Some of the notable works in this regard involve a local-global fusion approach by Lei et al. [14], the modified dual-luminance ar-chitecture introduced by Ren et al. [20], the DSen2 method of Lanaras et al. [13] specifically designed for Sentinel-2 imagery, and SWCGAN [23], an adversarial architecture based on Swin Transformer. 

Transformer-based Super-Resolution: As a solution to this problem, transformer networks have become popular recently for image restoration tasks because of the ability to learn both local and non-local interactions using shifted-window self-attention mechanisms introduced in the Swin Transformer architec-ture. As a result, SwinIR [15] and Swin2SR [2] architectures have set state-of-the-art benchmarks in the field of image restoration and super-resolution. Concerning transformer-based SR in the context of remote sensing, recent years have seen research along various lines of development, which include, but are not limited to, hybrid convolutional and transformer-based generator models [23], real-world degradation-aware architectures such as KANet [4], efficiency-oriented Swin models like ESTNet [11], and more sophisticated alternatives like mixture-of-expert feed-forward layers [21]. Among the above-mentioned approaches, the Swin2SR architecture is of the greatest interest to our research, as it relies on the hierarchy of the Swin backbone and an efficient image restoration procedure. Nevertheless, the FFN in each transformer block is a standard two-layer MLP that does not account for frequency-based image degradation. 

Frequency-Aware Restoration: The reconstruction of high-frequency in-formation like edges, textures, and fine structures is essential in the application 4 MA. Hossain et al. 

domain of remote sensing. It is widely reported that the restoration of high-frequency information is an open problem under realistic sensor blur and alias-ing [19]. To solve this problem, current works rely on perceptual losses, multi-branch refinement blocks, degradation-aware training processes [4], or generative models trained on extensive datasets. In contrast, only a small number of works have attempted to inject frequency-aware inductive bias into the transformer FFN directly. Another motivation for learning frequency-aware features stems from traditional SR models such as RCAN [26], which filter out dominant low-frequency information to focus on learning high-frequency residuals. In optical satellite imaging, degradation is mainly due to sensor blur, optical aberrations, and sampling effects. These observations motivate restoration models that incor-porate frequency-aware inductive biases, even when the underlying degradation process is not explicitly modeled. Unlike existing methods that rely on attention refinement, SFG-SwinSR fo-cuses on enhancing the Feed-Forward Network (FFN). We introduce a spatial frequency module that decomposes features via depthwise blurring, separating low-frequency structures from high-frequency residuals, which are then refined and gated. Our key innovation modifies the original Swin2SR attention block and improves detail recovery through FFN optimization.

3.4 Training Objective 

The model is trained using a detail-guided composite loss, summarized in Ta-ble 1, to jointly encourage pixel-level accuracy, structural fidelity, edge preser-vation, and frequency-domain consistency. The overall objective consists of four complementary components: pixel-wise L1 loss, SSIM loss, edge guidance loss, and frequency guidance loss. In our experiments, the weights are set to λL1 = 1 .0, λSSIM = 0 .1, λedge =0.1, and λf req = 0 .05 . # 4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Dataset Preparation 

The proposed experimental setup employs two separate datasets to assess the performance in both simulated and realistic scenarios. 

SpaceNet (WorldView-2) . The main source of training data is the SpaceNet [24] WorldView-2-derived orthorectified RGB product at approximately 30 cm spa-tial resolution. Given that the corresponding native low-resolution (LR) images are not provided in this dataset, the LR-HR image pairs are synthesized via 8 MA. Hossain et al. 

the proposed degradation process outlined in Figure 2. To conduct a robust ex-periment, the entire dataset is shuffled with a fixed random seed of 42. After removing fully black image pairs, we obtain 12,144 valid image pairs, comprising 10,261 training samples and 1,883 test samples. 

SEN2VEN μS Dataset . For evaluating generalization on actual cross-sensors data, we consider the SEN2VEN μS version 2 dataset [18]. The SEN2VEN μSdataset comprises Sentinel-2 patches at resolutions of 10 m and 20 m, with their corresponding reference patches from the VEN μS satellite, acquired within a 30-minute window at 5 m resolution. In total, there are 132,955 patches present in the entire dataset; however, we randomly select subset of 20,000 images to optimize computational load. This subset is divided into 18,000 training images (10% for validation) and a fixed set of 2,000 images for final testing. During training, we extract random aligned spatial crops across both datasets. For SpaceNet, patch dimensions are set to 64 × 64 for LR and 128 × 128 for HR, corresponding to a 2× scale factor. For SEN2VEN μS, we accommodate both 2×

and 4× scale factors: 2× super-resolution utilizes LR patches of 128 × 128 and HR patches of 256 × 256 , while 4× super-resolution uses LR patches of 64 × 64 

and HR patches of 256 × 256 . To isolate and objectively evaluate the structural representation capacity of the networks, no spatial data augmentations (such as flipping or rotation) are applied during training.

4.3 Implementation Details 

Both Swin2SR and the proposed SFG-SwinSR are implemented in PyTorch and trained end-to-end on a single NVIDIA A100-PCIE-40GB GPU. Unless other-wise stated, we optimize all models using AdamW with an initial learning rate of 2 × 10 −4 and weight decay of 1 × 10 −4. Training is conducted for 200 epochs with a batch size of 16 using a cosine annealing schedule (minimum learning rate of 1 × 10 −6). For reproducibility, a fixed random seed of 42 is used for data shuffling and weight initialization. SFG-SwinSR adopts the Swin2SR backbone and modifies the feed-forward network (FFN) within each transformer block. The model is configured with win-dow size 8, embedding dimension C = 180 , six stages with depths [6 , 6, 6, 6, 6, 6] 

(36 blocks total), and six attention heads per block, with an MLP expansion ratio of r = 2 .0. In each block, the standard FFN is replaced by the proposed SFG-FFN, parameterized by a depthwise blur kernel size k = 5 and reduction ratio ρ = 8 . All other components like the shifted-window partitioning, and re-construction head, are kept identical to Swin2SR to isolate the impact of the proposed frequency-gated FFN design. 

# 5 Results and Discussion 

5.1 Quantitative Results 

Experiments are evaluated on fixed test sets from SpaceNet and SEN2VEN μS. Table 2 compares the proposed SFG-SwinSR against Swin2SR and Swin2-MoSE under identical evaluation settings. On the SpaceNet dataset, SFG-SwinSR has the best overall performance with 45.19 dB PSNR, 0.9852 SSIM, and 0.0031 

MAE. Compared to the Swin2SR baseline, the proposed method improves PSNR by +1.63 dB , increases SSIM by +0.0072 , and reduces MAE by approximately 

25.81% . Compared with Swin2-MoSE, SFG-SwinSR achieves a +0.58 dB 

PSNR improvement while maintaining comparable structural similarity. These results indicate that the proposed spatial-frequency-gated FFN enhances detail reconstruction and feature refinement in remote sensing super-resolution. On the SEN2VEN μS benchmark, SFG-SwinSR achieves the highest PSNR values at both scaling factors, reaching 49.35 dB for ×2 super-resolution and 

45.52 dB for ×4 super-resolution. The proposed model consistently outperforms Swin2SR by +0.92 dB and +1.4 dB PSNR at ×2 and ×4 scales, respectively. In terms of SSIM, SFG-SwinSR maintains competitive structural reconstruction quality while achieving lower MAE values, demonstrating improved pixel-level fidelity and cross-sensor generalization capability across different resolutions.

5.4 Qualitative Analysis 

The SFG-SwinSR model aims to enhance the recovery of fine structural details like road borders, roof contours, and repeating urban structures without causing unwanted noise enhancement in uniform areas (such as water surfaces and wide open spaces). The spatial-frequency gating module is introduced specifically for segregating and selectively amplifying high-frequency features, allowing for sharp yet stable reconstruction results. Visual inspections on the SpaceNet (Figure 3) and SEN2VEN μS (Figure 4) data confirm better building boundaries, finer edge detection, and less visual artifacts than those obtained by the base Swin2SR model. The experimental results support a focused and technically defensible con-clusion. SFG-SwinSR with the total loss outperforms the original L1-trained Swin2SR baseline and slightly surpasses the strongest Swin2SR configuration (L1+SSIM) as well. The gain is modest but directionally consistent across all three metrics (PSNR, SSIM, and MAE). Two key conclusions emerge from these results. First, explicit spatial-frequency decomposition within the transformer FFN is a beneficial inductive bias for re-mote sensing SISR, providing a more targeted detail recovery mechanism than the FFN. Second, the architectural modification and loss function design are complementary: the full benefit of SFG-FFN is only realized under structured supervision, as plain L1 alone is insufficient for the frequency-gated FFN to con-verge effectively. The contribution is therefore a focused refinement of Swin2SR rather than a claim of broad architectural superiority. 

5.5 Limitations 

This study has several limitations that warrant discussion. First, while SFG-SwinSR achieves a substantial improvement over the Swin2SR baseline (+1.63 dB on SpaceNet), the margin over Swin2-MoSE is more modest (+0.72 dB), suggesting that the frequency-gated FFN design may reach diminishing returns when competing against models that already incorporate architectural optimiza-tions for remote sensing. # 6 Conclusion 

This paper introduced SFG-SwinSR, a novel Spatial-Frequency Gated Swin Transformer designed for remote sensing image super-resolution. Our approach preserves the original Swin2SR attention mechanism while replacing its feed-forward network with a specialized spatial-frequency gated module that: (1) esti-mates low-frequency content via depthwise blurring, (2) extracts high-frequency residuals through subtraction, (3) refines these residuals using a lightweight spa-tial branch, and (4) adaptively reinjects details via bottleneck gating. 14 MA. Hossain et al. 

Extensive experiments across two challenging benchmarks demonstrate SFG-SwinSR’s effectiveness. On SpaceNet/WorldView-2 (1,883 test pairs), our model achieves 45.19 dB PSNR and 0.9852 SSIM, surpassing Swin2SR by +1.63 dB 

and Swin2-MoSE by +0.72 dB . On SEN2VEN μS, SFG-SwinSR establishes new consistent improvement with 49.35 dB PSNR at ×2 scale and 45.52 dB 

PSNR at ×4 scale, demonstrating consistent improvements of +0.92 dB and 

+1.4 dB over the baseline, respectively. These results validate our core hypoth-esis that explicit spatial-frequency feature transformation within transformer FFNs, when combined with structured detail-guided supervision, provides an ef-fective approach for remote sensing super-resolution across different sensors and scale factors. Future work will focus on evaluating SFG-SwinSR on additional real-world remote sensing datasets with diverse sensor characteristics and degradation pat-terns. We will also investigate dual-image remote sensing super-resolution by fusing two half-pixel-shifted observations to recover sub-pixel information and improve high-resolution reconstruction.
