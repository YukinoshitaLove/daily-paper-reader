## Abstract 

The neural-network denoising functions which form the backbone of image dif-fusion models are remarkably consistent in their generalization behaviour across a wide variety of network architectures and training procedure hyperparameters. A recent line of research has sought to model the outputs of these networks by aggregating posterior weighted averages of training dataset patches. In this work, we consolidate these approaches into a unified model class which we call Filtered Posterior Mean Collections (FPMCs). We define this model class using query precision vectors, response weights, and source distributions, and illustrate that existing methods are recoverable with specific choices of these design axes. In-vestigating each axis in turn, we find that FPMC performance can be improved with soft relaxations of prior patch-based methods, and through augmentations of source distributions. Applying these findings to an existing FPMC, we demonstrate consistent sample improvement across three natural image datasets. 

## 1 Introduction 

Diffusion models [ 24 , 8, 26 ] are a powerful class of generative models, especially in the domain of visual data where they have been widely adopted for image [ 21 ] and video [ 7] generation. These models generate samples using a neural-network parameterized denoising function which is trained to recover clean data from those corrupted by varying amounts of additive Gaussian noise. The unique minimizer of this denoising score matching objective [ 29 ] is the optimal denoiser, a simple posterior weighted average over the elements of the training dataset [ 13 ]. However, this minimizer is a poor generative model that is incapable of generalization beyond the empirical training distribution [6]. The generalization ability of diffusion models therefore implies that network denoisers learn biased approximations of the optimal denoiser [ 32 , 25 ]. In the image domain, this bias is remarkably consis-tent across a wide variety of hyperparameters [ 34 ]. This observation has led to two complementary lines of research. The first of these seeks to understand the inductive biases responsible for denoiser convergence [ 9 , 20 , 10 , 1, 27 ], while the second aims to characterize the functional form of the resultant biased denoiser through network-free analytical approximations [23, 30, 19, 16, 10, 18]. Among analytical approaches, several methods have sought to approximate denoiser bias through posterior weighted averages of training dataset patches. These include Niedoba et al. [20] who propose patches based on average network gradients, Kamb and Ganguli [10] who utilize square patches and equivariant groups based on the inductive biases of convolutional networks, and Lukoianov et al. [18] who derive patches from the eigendecomposition of the data covariance. Together, these methods represent the most accurate analytical approximations of network denoiser behaviour currently

In this work, we directly address this limitation by consolidating prior approaches into a unified model class which we call filtered posterior mean collections (FPMCs). Our framework defines FPMCs using sequences of query precisions Q which filter the posterior means, response weights

Together, these three principal axes differentiate prior methodologies, and form the foundation for systematic improvement of model performance. Specifically, we improve FPMC denoisers by learning soft relaxations of prior patch-based methodologies, and through data augmentation strategies of their source distributions. When applied together, these modifications consistently improve the FPMC sample similarity versus diffusion model outputs on three natural image datasets. Our contributions are summarized as follows: 1. ## 3 Filtered Posterior Mean Collections

## 4 Improving FPMCs 

In this section, we investigate the principal design axes of FPMCs, identifying the limitations of prior choices and proposing improvements along each axis. 

4.1 Learning Soft Q

The choice of Q is the central decision of FPMC design. Prior art has primarily used Q to imbue their FPMCs with a local inductive bias – supposing that model outputs for a given pixel should be correlated more strongly to spatially nearby input pixels. While motivations for this inductive bias are grounded in network gradients [ 20 ], model architectures [ 10 ], and covariance eigendecomposition [18], all choices of Q in prior methods share two key characteristics. The first shared characteristic is a restriction of q to {0, 1}d. This choice corresponds to ˆμ which are averaged over patches of the dimensions of Rd. As illustrated in fig. 1, we argue that this restriction is inconsistent with the locality motivation of prior art as it requires every dimension within a patch to provide an equally weighted contribution to the posterior weights, regardless of spatial proximity. Secondly, prior art uses manually tuned heuristics to derive the patches in Q. Both Niedoba et al. [20] and Kamb and Ganguli [10] draw inspiration from convolutional receptive fields, selecting q

corresponding to square image patches. However, hyperparameter tuning is needed to select the patch size st. Even when Q is derived from data as in PSPC-Flex [ 20 ] and Lukoianov et al. [18] , binary q

vectors require a clipping function and corresponding threshold hyperparameter τt.We address the limitations of both characteristics. As presented in section 3, we relax the space of

Taken together, the performance of fine-tuned FPMCs supports our claim that soft, optimized Q are preferable to binary, heuristically derived alternatives. 

4.2 Learning Soft R

The purpose of R is to define how the L posterior mean estimators of an FPMC are aggregated to form a single output. As with the query precisions, all prior methods constrain their response weights to rℓ ∈ { 0, 1}d. Considering the methodologies of Kamb and Ganguli [10] and Lukoianov et al. [18] ,this is a natural restriction as both works propose R such that P 

> ℓ

rl = 1d. That is, each ˆμℓ estimator directly estimates a disjoint subset of the output dimensions. By contrast, Niedoba et al. [20] set 

R = Q, and propose a simple uniform average over all overlapping output means. We suggest that neither approach is ideal. Partitioning Rd through R ignores that estimators with similar query precisions qℓ induce similar likelihoods ˜p(z | x; qℓ), and therefore correlated ˆμℓ.Aggregation of such correlated estimators may improve the final estimate. However, uniform aggregation ignores the degree of correlation between estimators, treating all overlapping estimators as equally informative. We argue that better values of R lie between these choices. As with Q, we propose a relaxation of rℓ

from {0, 1}d to Rd

> ≥0

, and a data-driven selection of R through minimization of eq. (11) with respect to R. We consider the effect of fine-tuning R from the baseline values described in table 1 for the same FPMCs considered in section 4.1. Mirroring our optimization of Q, we freeze the other FPMC parameters to their baseline Q values and use the modified V discussed in section 4.1 The effect of fine-tuning soft R, is shown in the bottom row of fig. 2. Across all three FPMCs, fine-tuning R reduces FPMC error vs the DDPM++ denoiser. Among FPMCs, the most pronounced improvement is observed for Lukoianov et al. [18] supports our claim that averaging related estimators can lead to improved FPMC performance. 

4.3 V Augmentations 

The choice of source image distributions V is the least explored axis of FPMC design. Among prior methods, only the ELS model [ 10 ] does not exclusively use ν = pD (x). Motivated by the equivariant inductive bias of convolutions, ELS differs from the related LS model solely through the use of per-estimator translation-augmented ν. However, diffusion generalization consistency is not limited to purely convolutional architectures. Similar outputs are also produced by attention-enabled U-Nets and DiTs [20], neither of which are translation equivariant due to their use of attention layers [28]. Nevertheless, translational transformations are commonly employed in generative model training as a part of a broader class of data augmentation strategies. Such augmentations improve model training by expanding the empirical training distribution pD (x) with plausible synthetic images to better approximate the true data generating distribution p(x). We hypothesize that applying similar data augmentation to the source distributions of FPMC will also improve model performance. We therefore investigate augmenting V with other classical augmentation strategies, testing augmentation via translation, scaling, rotations, and reflections across the horizontal and vertical axes. However, as patch sizes increase with t, globally implausible augmentations become harmful. Horizontal reflections and synthetic samples remain plausible at all scales, explaining their consistent benefit.

Among augmentation strategies, synthetic augmentation outperforms horizontal reflection consistently. Examining fig. 4 however, we see that our FPMC is qualitatively very similar to its PSPC-Flex baseline, emphasizing that more research is required to close the gap to neural network samples. 

## 6 Related Work 

Diffusion Model Generalization The denoising objective of eq. (3) admits the optimal denoiser as its closed-form minimizer [ 13 ], but this denoiser is incapable of generalization [ 6]. Yoon et al. [33] demonstrate generalization occurs only when networks fail to memorize, a phenomenon which is driven by model capacity and dataset size [ 33 , 6]. Yi et al. [32] demonstrate that generalization emerges from biased estimators of the optimal score, which they attribute to optimization bias. Zhang et al. [34] demonstrate that this bias is remarkably consistent across a variety of hyperparameters. A line of research has sought to investigate sources of this bias, such as model architecture [ 10 , 9], training distribution [20, 25], or loss stochasticity [27, 19, 1]. 

Analytic Denoisers Many classical methods aim to recover images corrupted by moderate amounts of Gaussian noise [ 22 , 5, 3]. Of these, the Wiener filter is particularly relevant in the diffusion context. Research has found that these optimal linear denoisers are effective approximators of diffusion models [ 30 , 16 ], echoing findings that show diffusion generalization can be approximated using shrinkage operations on the eigenspace of network jacobians instead of data covariance matrices [ 9 ]. Lukoianov et al. [18] bridges these findings, connecting average network denoiser gradients and Wiener filters. A distinct line of work proposes analytical denoisers which directly approximate diffusion model generalization through modifications to the optimal denoiser. One approach smooths the optimal score function to produce a generalizing denoiser [ 23 ]. The focus of this work are methods based on the aggregation of multiple modified posterior means [ 20 , 10 , 18 ]. ## 7 Conclusions and Future Work 

The consistent generalization of neural-network image denoiser functions has motivated a growing line of research which aims to analytically approximate network denoisers. Among these, methods which aggregate posterior means over patches of the data have been particularly successful. In this work, we propose a unified filtered posterior mean collection framework which encompasses these fully network-free diffusion approximators. We investigate the design space of the model class, relaxing prior binary choices of Q and R through optimization, and identifying beneficial strategies to augment V. When applied jointly, these changes consistently improve FPMC sample similarity across three datasets. More broadly, we argue the systematic strategy employed by this work demonstrates the value of a unified FPMC framework which clearly delineates directions for future research. Despite the improvements proposed in this work, there remain substantial limitations. Chief among these, there remains a substantial gap between FPMCs and network denoisers, at the level of both denoiser and sample similarity as demonstrated by fig. 2 and table 2 respectively. Additionally, FPMCs are substantially less computationally efficient than their network counterparts. Regarding our contributions, although our method improves upon a PSPC-Flex baseline, in most cases, it does not improve MSE on FFHQ-64 × 64 , and falls short of state of the art for AFHQ r2. Moreover, evaluation in this work is performed solely on datasets which generally have a single subject. More complex image content may require further advancements beyond what we have proposed. The limitations discussed motivate several promising directions for FPMC development. The number of estimators L in the FPMC is an implicit, unexplored design axis. Prior methods make widely varying choices of L, suggesting that systematic investigation of the optimal collection size may be warranted. In addition, relaxing the structure of the FPMC framework may produce further benefit. From eq. (8), current FPMCs have only investigated diagonal query precision matrices. More expressive precision matrices which incorporate important correlations between dimensions may lead to greater improvements. Going further, moving from Gaussian likelihoods to non-linear kernels may better approximate the non-linear behaviour of neural-network parameterized denoisers. Finally, while this work adapts the query precisions and response weights based on t, adapting FPMCs based on the input z represents a natural extension which is likely to be especially useful for images which have discrete latent structure. 9Acknowledgements

## B Augmentation Strategies 

In this section, we detail the augmentation utilized in section 4.3. We first give an overview of how we generate our agumented datasets, and then detail each agumentation strategy. For the classical augmentation strategies, we use the augmentation pipeline of Karras et al. [13] , which was adapted from Karras et al. [12] . The augmentation pipeline is a stochastic generator which produces tuples of augmented images and labels which describe the parameters used to generate the augmentation transformation. Although the augmentation pipeline can be configured produce a composition of multiple geometric augmentations, we restrict our investigation to generating images with one augmentation strategy at a time. For each augmentation strategy, excluding the reflection transforms where only one augmentation per source image is possible, we first generate 1 million augmented images by sampling 20 augmentations per original training set image. When sampling augmented images using classical augmentation strategies, we keep a hash of the generated augmentation labels and discard any duplicate augmenta-tions. From the original augmented dataset of 1 million images, we produce smaller augmentation sets through random sub sampling. For dataset sizes which are integer multiples of |D| , we randomly select an equal amount of augmented images per source training image. For augmentation sets which are smaller than the original dataset size, we select one augmented image each for a subset of the original training images. The following augmentation strategies were explored 

Horizontal Reflection For this reflection, we simply flip the image horizontally. 

Vertical Reflection For this reflection, we simply flip the image vertically. 

Translation We translate images according to random offsets sampled as 

δx = ϵx · W

8 , ϵx ∼ N (0 , 1) (92) 

δy = ϵy · H

8 , ϵy ∼ N (0 , 1) . (93) Translated images are padded with reflection padding post-transformation. 20 Rotation The amount of rotation expressed in radians is randomly sampled from the uniform distribution 

θ ∼ U (−π, π ) (94) Like translations, images are padded with reflection padding to retain the original shape of the image post transformation. 

Scaling Scaling up or downsamples the image to resolution sH × sW . The amount of scaling is sampled from the log normal distribution 

ln( s) ∼ N (0 , 0.2) (95) and reflection padding is used when s < 1.

Synthetic Unlike prior augmentations, synthetic augmentations are not produced through transfor-mations of the source dataset images. Instead, sample our synthetic dataset using the EDM DDPM++ denoiser, and Huen sampling, following the same sampling procedure outlined in section C.4. Impor-tantly, while we generate our synthetic samples from using PF-ODE integration, we are careful to use a different random seed. Therefore sampling is initialized from a different set of initial z ∼ π(z) as the seeds used sample evaluation in table 2.
