## Abstract 

We construct a new kind of encoder, leveraging the expressive power of diffu-sion models. In a traditional variational autoencoder, the encoder and decoder jointly negotiate a latent representation of the input. This is made possible by the reparameterization trick, which simplifies training at the cost of restricting the encoder to a simple family of distributions. Replacing this encoder with a diffusion model requires rethinking how the decoder pressure can be transmitted back to the encoder, given that they tend to update their internal estimates of the latent in opposing directions. We solve this problem with an alternating training scheme, inspired by the expectation-maximization algorithm. Our method enables more reliable synchronization between encoder and decoder, while preserving the simple and efficient training objective of standard diffusion models. 

## 1 Introduction 

Can a diffusion model be used as an encoder? After all, diffusion models are highly expressive probabilistic models, which makes them a natural candidate for parameterizing complex distributions over latent variables. The challenge is that these models are typically applied to cases where the target distribution is fixed, whereas a latent distribution must necessarily evolve during training as the encoder and decoder negotiate an optimal representation of the input signal. In variational autoencoders (VAEs) [ 1], the encoder is a Gaussian distribution with a diagonal covariance. Although this choice limits representational flexibility, it enables joint training of the encoder and decoder through the reparameterization trick. That is, it is straightforward to express the latents in terms of the encoder parameters, which allows the latter to be directly coupled to the decoder pressure. The same is not true for a diffusion model, since the latents are generated through many more iterations over the underlying score network, which makes their functional dependence on the network parameters more complicated [ 2 ]. Hence, we need to resort to a training algorithm that alternates between encoder and decoder updates, similar to techniques commonly used in maximum entropy reinforcement learning [3, 4]. Sacrificing the convenience of joint training may still be worthwhile if it yields a more flexible latent representation. The limited expressiveness of a rigid encoder family can hinder the autoencoder‚Äôs effectiveness in the following ways: (1) the optimal encoder may not lie within the chosen family, (2) an overly smooth encoder may map semantically distinct inputs to overlapping neighborhoods in latent space, and (3) an inexpressive encoder can throttle the reconstruction fidelity of the decoder [ 5]. These effects are discussed in greater detail in Sec. 2. Part of the expressive power of a diffusion model comes from its iterative generative process, which allows the model to gradually build up complex internal correlations in the generated sample (see Sec. 3). Normalizing flows [ 6] and neural ODEs [ 7 ] also share this feature, but diffusion models benefit from a considerably simpler and more robust training objective (see Sec. 5). Flow matching models [ 8 ] also inherit this property. However, diffusion models remain differentiated by their firm grounding in information theory, specifically, their ability to efficiently estimate quantities like mutual

This is particularly relevant to autoencoders, which are, at their core, realizations of an information bottleneck [13‚Äì15]. In this paper, we develop a framework to leverage a diffusion model as an encoder. The key innovation is a training scheme that updates the encoder and decoder in alternation, while keeping them synchronized with one another (see Sec. 4). This procedure is directly inspired by the expectation-maximization algorithm [ 16 ], where the E-step updates a diffusion model with samples from the posterior, and the M-step updates the decoder. The posterior samples are generated from a short Langevin chain that explores the decoder landscape, while an affine drift term keeps the samples regularized. Lifting the regularization pressure from the encoder side subdues the tendency for the encoder and decoder to fall out of alignment with one another during their independent updates. The diffusion encoder takes on a more passive role in this arrangement, which means it can be trained with the standard, well-established denoising objective. Finally, it is important to be clear about the scope of this work. Our goal is not to surpass the state-of-the-art in encoder design; over a decade of refinement has made the Gaussian encoder a highly competitive baseline. Rather, we seek to motivate a new application of diffusion models that draws on their representational capacity and training simplicity [ 17 ‚Äì19 ]. Therefore, the experiments in Sec. 6 serve to demonstrate proof of concept‚Äîshowing that the proposed setup trains stably and produces coherent reconstructions‚Äîrather than optimizing reconstruction quality at scale. We will be transparent about the limitations of our framework as they arise, and suggest potential avenues for improvement throughout. We hope these ideas spur further research on the diffusion encoder.

## 6 Experiments 

We test our autoencoder on four different image datasets: MNIST, CIFAR-10, TinyImageNet, and CelebA-HQ, the last two scaled to 64 √ó 64 pixels [ 32 ‚Äì 36 ]. Hyperparameter choices for these 8experiments are given in Table 1. Qualitative samples and their reconstructions are provided in Figs. 5 to 8. We were especially alert to training instabilities, which is precisely what Algorithm 1 is designed to counter (see Sec. A.1). Empirically, we observed no significant stability issues, even across varying hyperparameter regimes. Moreover, the loss profiles were smooth and nearly monotonic, consistent with an EM-like training scheme (see Fig. 7a). This indicates that the encoder and decoder evolved in lockstep rather than competing with one another. Such synchronization is noteworthy, given that even in a standard VAE, the encoder can lag behind the decoder, leading to posterior collapse [22]. Next, we evaluate our autoencoder using rate-distortion (RD) curves, which characterize the funda-mental tradeoff between compression and reconstruction accuracy [ 15 ]. For a VAE, rate refers to the regularization term from Eq. (6), and for the diffusion encoder, we use its neural entropy as a close proxy (cf. Eq. (32)). Distortion measures the fidelity of the output. We use a decoder with a standard MSE loss in our experiments, so distortion corresponds to the average squared distance between the input and its reconstruction (cf. Eq. (42)). In short, RD curves trace the equilibrium between the mutual information terms in the information bottleneck, Eq. (1), over a range of temperatures Œ≥.Fig. 4 shows the RD curves for a VAE and dAE. The latter refers to our diffusion encoder + convolutional decoder setup. The VAE uses the same decoder, paired with a symmetric convolutional encoder (see Sec. E.6). RD curves positioned closer to the origin represent superior performance, as they achieve lower reconstruction error at a given rate. Therefore, it is clear from Fig. 4 that the classic VAE fares better than the dAE. However, it reveals that the dAE operates very close to the VAE‚Äôs RD frontier, despite having a fundamentally different approach to posterior approximation. The VAE encoder directly optimizes the quantities being plotted in Fig. 4, namely Lrec and Lreg from Eqs. (4) and (6). The diffusion encoder learns the optimal solution q‚ãÜ(z|x) from its samples. But the VAE encoder offers better performance, despite the fact that it is a Gaussian (Eq. (2)). To understand this better, we return to the toy model from Sec. 2, and train a diffusion encoder + polynomial decoder on its samples {x}. We find that the diffusion encoder picks up on the z ‚Üí ‚àí z symmetry of the posterior, but still produces a nearly Gaussian qŒ∏ (z|x) even though it can capture the true p(z|x) (see Fig. 9). We wonder if an analogous statement is true in the general case, and whether that implies some form of universality of the Gaussian encoder. Of course, this is not the only explanation. First, neural entropy only upper-bounds the rate in the diffusion encoder case, so there might be a gap between the quantities we are using on the x-axis for the VAE and DAE RD curves. Second, there may be some additional source of error in modeling 

q‚ãÜ(z|x) from its samples that adds to the distortion. Given the intricacy of the diffusion encoder, it is probable that multiple factors contribute to this discrepancy. We see this as fertile ground for continued research. 

## 7 Conclusion 

Can a diffusion model be used as an encoder? Yes, absolutely. Is there more work to be done before it is ready for primetime? Also yes. The main contribution of our paper is Algorithm 1, which enables (1) synchronized training of a decoupled encoder and decoder pair, and (2) training of the diffusion encoder with the standard denoising objective. A key enabler of our diffusion encoder is the architecture described in Sec. E, which progressively injects information from the input into the latent over the course of the denoising process. We do not claim this to be an optimal design, and expect that more careful architectural choices would yield further improvements. The primary weakness of Algorithm 1 is the sequential overhead introduced by the Langevin chains‚Äî patience is the price we pay for stability. Here again, there is room for improvement, by adaptive adjustment of nsteps as training settles, and/or the use of critically damped dynamics to reach the stationary distribution more rapidly. We elaborate on these ideas in Sec. A.2. Fortunately, for lower-dimensional X training times remain reasonable; this makes our setup a strong candidate for certain maximum entropy reinforcement learning problems [3, 4]. In closing, we note that our training scheme exhibits a certain resilience to posterior collapse, as noted in Sec. 6. This makes sense, since Algorithm 1 is explicitly designed to accommodate a certain amount of lag from the encoder, allowing the encoder to catch up with the decoder if it sprints ahead. Whether this structural robustness has broader applications beyond our current setting remains an intriguing open question. 9References

A.2 Latency 

The biggest drawback with Algorithm 1 is the latency induced by sequential iteration of the posterior SDE, Eq. (21), each step of which involves taking a derivative of the decoder network. As mentioned there, nsteps does not have to be large for the Langevin chain to equilibrate, since the encoder places the initial latents close to their final destination. We typically set nsteps = 10 in our experiments (see Table 1). This parameter is what allows the setup to tolerate some slack between the encoder and decoder, as the latter races ahead in step 11. Even though there is no regularization pressure on the encoder side, the encoder lags because it was last updated with a z‚ãÜ that may no longer reflect the decoder‚Äôs preference at the end of each cycle. The lag is largest early in training, when the decoder loss landscape is changing most rapidly. As the losses stabilize, the encoder catches up and tracks the decoder more closely. It also means we no longer need as many nsteps for the latents to settle to z‚ãÜ. In fact, it is possible to adaptively lower 

nsteps as training progresses with little to no effect on synchronization. We did this experiment, but there was a catch: we used JAX as our ML framework [ 37 ], and it requires a separate JIT-compilation of the model for each nsteps setting. For smaller datasets, this offsets the latency improvements from adaptive nsteps , but cleverer ML engineers than us should be able to resolve this. Another possibility is to introduce a velocity variable into the Langevin dynamics, imbuing zœÑ with inertia. This smoothens the evolution of zœÑ , which (1) regulates it from making large jumps when it encounters regions with large ‚àázœÑ log pœà (x|zœÑ ), and (2) leads to faster equilibration if the dynamics is tuned correctly. We did not try this ourselves, but we state the idea here for future development.

The decoder maps a latent vector z back to image space through a sequence of convolutional upsampling stages. The latent is first projected by a dense layer to a small spatial grid, which is then progressively upsampled using transposed convolutions, each followed by a residual refinement block. Optionally, self-attention blocks with layer normalization can be inserted at low-resolution feature maps to capture long-range spatial dependencies during upsampling. A final transposed convolutional layer maps the feature map to the output image dimensions, with a sigmoid activation to constrain pixel values to [0 , 1] .
