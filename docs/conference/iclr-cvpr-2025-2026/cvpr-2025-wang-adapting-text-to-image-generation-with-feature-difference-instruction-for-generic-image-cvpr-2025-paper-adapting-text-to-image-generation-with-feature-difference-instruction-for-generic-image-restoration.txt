## Abstract 

Diffusion-based Text-to-Image (T2I) models have demon-strated significant potential in image restoration. However, existing models continue to grapple with challenges such as complex training and prompt design. We introduce a new perspective for improving image restoration by inject-ing knowledge from pretrained vision-language models into current T2I models. We empirically show that the degra-dation and content representations in BLIP-2 can be lin-early separated, providing promising degradation guidance for image restoration. Specifically, the Feature Difference Instruction (FDI) is first extracted by Q-Formers through a simple subtraction operation based on reference image pairs. Then, we propose a multi-scale FDI adapter to de-couple the degradation style and corrupted artifacts, and inject the styleflow exclusively into specific blocks through adapter-tuning, thereby preventing noise interference and eschewing the need for cumbersome weight retraining. In this way, we can train various task-specific adapters ac-cording to different degradations, achieving rich detail en-hancement in the restoration results. Furthermore, the pro-posed FDI adapters have attractive properties of practi-cal value, such as composability and generalization abil-ity for all-in-one and mixed-degradation restoration. Ex-tensive experiments under various settings demonstrate that our method has promising repairing quality over 10 image restoration tasks and a wide range of other applications. 

## 1. Introduction 

As a classical yet challenging Computer Vision (CV) task for many real-world applications, image restoration aims to recover high-quality images from degraded inputs by re-moving rain [19, 27, 63], haze [22, 64, 84], snow [10, 11, 42], low-light [36, 70, 90], etc . These traditional methods typically concentrate on specific, pre-defined weather con-ditions. To improve model scalability, researchers have pro-posed all-in-one networks [12, 31, 38, 45, 77] capable of handling different types of degradations through combined model ensembles. However, such approaches often necessi-tate separate training for each distinct condition or struggle with multiple degradations within a single image. Aiming at this problem, several works [21, 79, 80] have been proposed to restore images with mixed corruptions in a blind setting,

Meanwhile, recent advancements in large Multimodal Vision-Language Models (MVLMs) have dramatically im-proved the capability to process and interpret visual and tex-tual information in tandem. These models, which lever-age both vision and language modalities, are integral to a range of applications, from understanding models for image captioning [34, 39, 61, 82, 98], visual question answering [4, 13, 33, 35, 74], to generating models for text-guided image creation [20, 52, 62, 66, 69] and edit-ing [6, 8, 49, 50, 97]. Taking into account both effective-ness and efficiency, how to stably and reliably combine the strengths of both understanding and generating models for downstream CV tasks becomes intractable. Several studies leverage pretrained MVLMs for down-stream computer vision tasks. CLIPStyler [30] and In-stanStyle [81] propose to use CLIP-based guidance for style transfer, aligning input image content with target styles described by text prompts. Similar to CLIPStyler, DA-CLIP [45] and MPerceiver [3] further explore acquiring Degradation-Aware (DA) hints from the pretrained CLIP model and then inject the guidance into Text-to-Image (T2I) models for image restoration. However, two problems re-main: (1) Both studies heavily rely on the design of prompt generation based on input-degraded images, which leads to mutual interference between content and degradation within the visual embedding [56]; and, (2) For complex illumination-related degradations such as haze, their visual degradative effects cannot be accurately described solely through text descriptions [46]. In this paper, we aim to address these challenges for a more efficient and robust restoration framework, named 

DiffRes . We observe that BLIP-2 can effectively capture features encompassing both semantic content and degra-dation attributes, which are linearly separable to some ex-tent. Specifically, we find the feature difference between clean and degraded images from BLIP-2 can be interpreted by Large Language Models (LLMs) [17], and effectively used as degradation instructions/conditions for T2I mod-els, guiding image restoration during the denoising process (see Figure 2). Compared to past studied that utilize CLIP-based prompts derived from the input degraded image, our reference-image-pair-based Feature Difference Instruction (FDI) is easily accessible while being able to circumvent the impact of degradation factors during the CLIP image encoding, and encapsulate more directed DA-aware infor-mation through feature-level subtraction. Combined with the input image features, FDI provides more effective and efficient guidance for high-quality restoration. Building on this, we further design an adapter tuning-based framework that leverages FDI as a core component to enhance image restoration. Our framework, DiffRes , in-tegrates FDI within a pretrained T2I model, enabling it to precisely reconstruct images using the feature differences as restoration cues. DiffRes consists of an inversion-based T2I model for high-fidelity reconstruction, a multi-scale FDI adapter for precise restoring guidance, and a degrada-tion classifier for mixed degradation removal. During train-ing, the pretrained Stable Diffusion (SD) remains frozen, in which DDIM inversion projects the degraded image into the latent space of LDM to provide the content information. The adapters extract defacement-related guidance instruc-tions from FDI, such as color, depth, illumination, and styl-ization, and then inject them into the reverse diffusion pro-cess in a tuning way. A multi-label classifier enables com-posite degradation restoration and more customized image processing. By injecting the FDI within the denoising pro-cess, DiffRes addresses both semantic content and degrada-tion factors simultaneously. This design not only enhances restoration quality but also improves the model adaptability across various types of degradation, paving the way for a more versatile and robust restoration solution. Our contributions are threefold: • We show that the BLIP-2 feature differences between clean and degraded images have the potential to in-struct image restoration, with more targeted and clear Degradation-Aware (DA) guidance. • We propose Feature Difference Instruction (FDI) adapter, a more effective tuning-based method to inject the im-plicit DA instructions into pretrained SD in a low-cost feature addition manner. • Without loss of generalizability, we conduct extensive ex-periments under various settings to verify the superiority of DiffRes. Results show that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance over 10 image restoration tasks.

Understand-ing models such as CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training) [61], BLIP (Bootstrapped Language-Image Pre-training) [34], and BLIP-2 [35] advanced cross-modal un-derstanding by aligning visual and linguistic representa-tions. Leveraging these foundations, a recent study [30, 32, 45, 81, 89] explored their applications in computer vision tasks. For instance, InstantStyle [81] leverages CLIP by capturing style features through the difference in encoded features between images and text. Similarly, DEADiff [56] employs BLIP-2 to manually decouple style and content us-ing Q-formers during training. Meanwhile, inspired by the great success of diffusion-based vision-language generation models [52, 62, 66, 69], several recent text-to-image (T2I) methods [3, 40, 53, 68] have been proposed for both su-pervised and zero-shot computer vision tasks such as image stylization and super-resolution. In this paper, we propose DiffRes to efficiently com-bine two pre-trained MVLMs, i.e ., vision-language un-derstanding model BLIP-2 [35] and vision-language gen-erating model Stable Diffusion [66], for universal image restoration. Considering the restoration as a process of de-coupling degradation style and generating corrupted con-tent , we discover that the feature difference in BLIP-2 can be effectively applied to image restoration tasks as repairing instruction for pre-trained T2I models. 

Image Restoration While single degradation image restoration methods [19, 22, 27, 42, 64, 90] have received significant progress, recent studies [9, 12, 38, 45, 54] seek to use an all-in-one framework to handle different degrada-tions simultaneously. For instance, DA-CLIP [45] utilizes an additional image controller based on the pretrained CLIP model, which learns the degradation embeddings matched to the input image through contrastive learning. However, these methods can only repair one single type of degrada-tion every time during inference, making them inapplica-ble to real-world scenarios where multiple weather degrada-tions may coexist in a single image. To solve this challenge, several methods [3, 21, 79, 80] are proposed for mixed degradations removal. For example, Han et al . propose the Blind Image Decomposition (BID) [21] setting, regarding rain and other real-world weather corruptions as superim-posing and separable to a clean image. MPerceiver [3] de-signs a multimodal prompt-based network with a learnable degradation predictor for restoration. Luo et al . [46] fur-ther improved DA-CLIP by incorporating mixed degrada-tion labels into the dataset and optimizing the discrepancy between degraded and clean image embeddings through a fidelity loss. However, these methods still require tedious network training or prompt optimization against input noise interference, thus impeding the real-world implementation. In contrast, through efficient reference-based adapter tuning on pre-trained T2I models, our DiffRes enables both 23541 degradation-specific or -agnostic, and single- or mixed-degraded image restoration with high flexibility. Mean-while, benefiting from the multimodal understanding capa-bilities of BLIP-2 and the generative priors of pre-trained diffusion models, our method can also be extended to other image-to-image translation applications. 

## 3. Method 

3.1. Preliminary 

Our method is based on the Latent Diffusion Model (LDM), which operates in a compressed latent space to enable effi-cient generation. The input image is first encoded into a latent representation using a pretrained Variational Autoen-coder (VAE) [28]. The model then combines a forward dif-fusion process that incrementally adds noise to the latent representation and a reverse denoising process that recon-structs the original data. Based on this observa-tion, we provide customized alignment between the vision-language understanding models ( e.g ., BLIP-2) and generat-ing models ( e.g ., stable diffusion). Instead of retraining T2I models from scratch, we use adapter-tuning methods by in-tegrating external plugins. 3.4. FDI Adapter 

Noise Decoupling. Since FDI already contains the degrada-tion style and spatial information, we employ an inversion-based diffusion process to inpaint the corrupted areas. Consequently, the FDI, serving as the degradation or de facto restoration guidance, should ideally retain only degradation-related information. However, in practice, when dealing with complex degraded images, such as those severely obscured by rain or haze, the FDI still suffers from certain noise interference from the corrupted unpaired regions. To mitigate this problem, we propose empha-sizing the degradation-relevant features in FDI combined with a more comprehensive pixel-level image feature space. We employ cross-attention [78] to refine the input FDI F

(Query) based on the multi-stage image features AiS (Key, Value) extracted from the adapter. In this way, the refined output FDI ˆF iS become more focused on the degradation-related parts, enhancing the accuracy of subsequent image restoration or enhancement tasks. 

Multi-level Guidance. After the FDI refinement, we first inject the repairing guidance into the encoding layers of the denoising U-Net. Considering for low-level CV tasks, dif-ferent encoding channels focus on varying types of informa-tion [5, 93]. For example, in the style transfer field [16, 25], features at different levels typically represent different as-pects of style: lower-level features capture textures and de-tails, while higher-level features are more inclined towards 23542 overall color and abstract style. Thus, we utilize the op-timized FDI ˆF iS to further generate channel-wise weights based on the classical SEBlock [24], guiding the focus of information in each encoding layer through channel atten-tion. 3.5. Degradation Classifier 

In real-world scenarios, multiple types of degradation fre-quently co-occur, leading to the coexistence of various dis-tortions within a single image. For instance, an image may depict weather conditions involving both rain and fog si-multaneously. To address this complexity, we addition-ally deploy an MLP-based multi-label classifier to identify the degradation types present in the input image, as illus-trated in Figure 3 (b). This multi-label classifier enables our DiffRes model to perform both all-in-one and mixed-degradation removal with high flexibility from two perspec-tives. (1) Composite: Simultaneously employ multi-branch FDI adapters trained on mixed datasets with different degra-dation types. Each branch can be combined through direct addition with adjustable weights obtained from the classi-fier. In this setting, the input ˆF iS of our visual modulator is the mean value of each adapter branch. (2) Progressive: Sequentially apply different task-specific adapters. This ap-proach also allows for flexible customization to selectively remove specific weather effects, as shown in Figure 4.

## 4. Experimental Setup and Results 

Datasets. For the task-specific and task-agnostic settings, we consider 10 restoration tasks: deraining, raindrop re-moval, dehazing, desnowing, low-light enhancement, de-noising, motion deblurring, defocus deblurring, shadow re-moval, and JPEG decompression. For each degradation type, we collect data from the corresponding datasets, Rain: Rain100 (L&H) [88] and Rain1400 [19]; Haze: RESIDE-6K [58]; Snow: Snow100K [42]; Raindrop: RainDrop [57]; Low-light: LOL-v1 [83] and MEF [47]; Noise: DIV2K [2], Flickr2K [75] and CBSD68 [48]; Motion blur: GoPro [51]; Defocus blur: DPDD [1]; Shadow: SRD [59]; JPEG com-pression: DIV2K [2], Flickr2K [75] and LIVE1 [71]. For the mixed-degradation setting, we use RainDS [60] for rain-drop + rainstreak, NYU-Rain [37] and OutDoor-Rain [77] for rainstreak + haze + low-light, and BID [21] for more complex combinations. Note that during the testing stage, to ensure a fair comparison, we use the whole training set corresponding to the test set to obtain the average FDI. De-tails of the dataset selection under different task settings are provided in the Supp. 

Evaluation Metrics. Apart from the commonly used pixel-level metrics PSNR and SSIM, we also adopt a va-riety of widely used metrics to provide a comprehensive evaluation of the different methods. In terms of feature-level perceptual quality, we use FID [23] and LPIPS [99] as reference-based metrics to measure the distance and simi-larity between ground truth images and the restored images. 

Implementation Details. We choose the pre-trained SD V2.1-512 model with 50 sampling steps as our base model. During training, we employ the AdamW optimizer [43] (β1 = 0 .9, β2 = 0 .99 ) over 50 epochs with a learning rate of  1 × 10 −5. ## 5. Conclusion 

Image restoration is challenging, even with the advances of-fered by Diffusion-based Text-to-Image (T2I) models. We explore the linear separability of features in the pretrained BLIP-2, and its potential across various image restoration tasks. Combining this with the multi-scale features sub-ject to the input reference images, we propose a novel Feature Difference Instruction (FDI), to efficiently inject the degradation-aware guidance into the pretrained text-to-image models. Taking advantage of inversion-based diffu-sion and reference-based FDI adapter, we achieved a flex-ible tuning-based pipeline, namely DiffRes, to efficiently restore arbitrary hybrid degradations in a unified frame-work. To enable all-in-one and mixed-degradation learning, we further introduce an additional classifier providing ad-justable weights for the composable FDI adapter. Extensive experiments on 10 restoration tasks and other translation ap-plications demonstrate the effectiveness and superiority of our unified network over strong baselines. We hope that our method takes the research community one step closer to transferring the knowledge of vision-language models to more CV tasks in the future. 23546 Acknowledgment
